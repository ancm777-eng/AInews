🔭 Overview

The "legal wall" surrounding AI infrastructure has significantly tightened as Congress moves to address the systemic smuggling of American semiconductors. On Thursday, the House Foreign Affairs Committee voted 42-0 to pass the Chip Security Act (H.R. 3447), a bipartisan bill that proposes new federal mandates for the hardware-level policing of AI chips.

The decisive strategic shift is the requirement for "chip security mechanisms," including location-verification features, to be integrated into all advanced, export-controlled semiconductors. This legislation serves as a direct response to reports that companies like DeepSeek have bypassed existing controls to acquire restricted NVIDIA GPUs, as well as the recent indictment of three individuals—including Wally Liaw, a co-founder of Supermicro—for allegedly participating in a scheme to smuggle restricted AI servers to China.

The total scale of the alleged diversion reaches approximately $2.5 billion; the $510 million figure cited in some reporting represents only the portion allegedly transacted during a six-week window in April–May 2025 and does not reflect the full scope of the indictment. While the bill seeks to prevent the CCP from accelerating its AI capabilities with American technology, the semiconductor industry has expressed concerns that "blanket mandates" for untested on-chip mechanisms could undermine global trust in U.S. technology.

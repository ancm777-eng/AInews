🔭 Overview

The immense strain of the AI buildout on the U.S. power grid has officially escalated into a federal legislative issue. On April 11, Representative Paul Tonko introduced the "Power for the People Act" in the House, a bill designed to force data center developers to bear the financial burden of the electricity infrastructure they require.

This move accompanies a parallel effort already introduced in the Senate by Senator Chris Van Hollen and colleagues, indicating bipartisan momentum. The legislation directly targets the current practice where utilities fund massive grid upgrades to service new data centers and then pass those costs on to residential and commercial ratepayers. The scale of the issue is stark: data centers now account for 55% of all new electricity demand in the U.S. and are projected to consume up to 15% of the nation's total electricity by 2030. The underlying cost data that drove this legislation comes from a Union of Concerned Scientists analysis, which found that consumers in seven PJM-region states — Illinois, Maryland, New Jersey, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Virginia, and West Virginia — paid approximately $4.3 billion in 2024 for local transmission upgrades linked to data center connections. The "13 states" figure that sometimes appears in coverage refers to the total geographic footprint of the PJM grid operator, not the number of states where these specific costs were tallied. The bill would mandate that FERC create rules ensuring data centers pay for their associated transmission upgrades and would also establish a formal queue to manage new grid connections.
